Letter from Robert Carter to Edward Tucker, April 15, 1730

     Robert Carter writes to Weymouth merchant Edward Tucker, April 15, 1730, complaining about the low prices received for his tobacco, the good ale Tucker had sent but the poor packing of it, the tobacco he is shipping, and requesting the merchant find Carter's mining company an experienced copper miner.


Letter from Robert Carter to Edward Tucker, April 15, 1730

-1-

[Rappahannock, Lancaster County, Virginia]

Apl: 15. 1730

Edward Tucker Esqr

Sir

     I receiv'd your packets by Yr Ship Port
land CaptRussel with yr severall Accots of sales some
of them are very mean so low that at these rates we Shall
never be able to maintain our familys by our Labour
and yet I must own I fare no better in most other places

     The ale you sent me proves very good. I had
a prodigious damage in that which Came in the hamp:
ers. I must for the future desire what I have to be packt into
hhds the Charges will be the greater but find tis The much
safer way

     This comes by Jno Russel brings you a
bill of Lading for 10 hhds of Tobo. made at two of my
plantations it is all to one hhd that 20 odd hands made
there are some hhds of it that are Strip to promiscuously
into Luggs. I chose it as the better way to Avoid Shipping the
Crop trash and I think I can demonstrate that it is provide [d]
people are Carefull when we had the liberty of stemming
before We used to manage our under tobo this Way and I never
faild to have less than a 1/2 and 3/4 more for it a pound than
for the best leaf

     herein I send you a bill of Exch: drawn by
John Sharpley on yr Self for £5"1"3. which pleas to Accep
t or protest Pray remember my Accot Currt by Your Next altho by
a little reflection I may know how to Value my Self in yr books
I have yr favour in answer to mine from the freshes of our

-2-


River you give me no hopes of Sending me a Mason if you
could send me in good digger that hath been a Worker in the Copper
mines and knows how to follow a Vein under Ground it
will be of a great Service to us by the many signs we have met with &
the Tryals we have made we have Certainly found a ps of ground
That has Copper mines in it We have been at a Great deal of Charge
already and have rais'd some small parcels of very good ore but for want of men
of Skill our progress is but little in the design Perhaps it may
lye in the Way of Yr: business to procure us men fit for our work
We are in a Company upon this design Colo Page my two
Sons and my Self compose this Company you will go
near to have a joynt letter from us upon this Affair I am


Sir
Yor most Humble Servant

Enclosd is likewise a bill of Exchange drawn by Capt
John Russel on yr self for £4"19
4 letter
Russel

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Letter book, 1728 August-1731 July, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. That address as well as the county and colony have been added for clarity.

[1] John Russell commanded the Portland, a vessel owned by Weymouth merchant Edward Tucker. ( Survey Report 9729 detailing the Weymouth Port Books, Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.)
