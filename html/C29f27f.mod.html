Letter from Robert Carter to Micajah Perry, June 27, 1729

     Robert Carter writes to London merchant Micajah Perry, June 27, 1729, to alert him to bills of echange and a bill of lading for tobacco from the Lloyd estate.


Letter from Robert Carter to Micajah Perry, June 27, 1729

-1-

Rappahannock, [Lancaster County, Virginia]

June 27th. 1729

Micajah Perry Esqr.

Sir --

     This Accompanies the Bailey and brings to you
a bill of Lading for 4 hogsheads of the LLs being the remainder of that mark

     I now send you Some Second bills of Exchange amounting
to £82:7:3 the firsts went by the Spotswood. here is a first bill of Major
Bartlets on yourself for £10. I have already advised you of all the
bills of Exchange If I mistake not Colonel Page and my self hath drawn upon
you upon the Account of Mr. Burwells Children

-2-


     I hsve already written so much of the LLs affair that I have
nothing now to add


NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter Letter Book, 1727 April 13-1728 July 23, Carter Family Papers, Virginia Historical Society, Richmond.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity to the heading on the draft.

[1] The Bailey was a London ship owned by William Dawkins and commanded at various times by Adam Graves (1725-1730) and by Thomas Dove (1731-1732). She was a vessel of some 250 tons and carried 15-17 crew members. ( Survey report 6801 summarizing Adm. 68/195, 156v, and other data in Adm. 68/194 and /196, found in the microfilms of the Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia; A letter of Carter's executors to Dawkins 1738 May 10 refers to "your ship Bailey.")

[2] This London ship was commanded by James Bradby, 1727-1732. ( Adm 68/195, 70r ff., found in the microfilms of the Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.)
