Summary



Letter from Robert Carter to John Savage, April 13, 1724

     Robert Carter writes to John Savage, April 13, 1724, to repeat and emphasize the instructions for surveys Savage is to make for Carter on the colony's frontier. He reminds Savage that Thomas Barber is to assist with the work, and Carter is sending to Savage by Barber some deeds for lands in the area of Bull Run. Some surveys were made in the area ten years earlier, but Savage is to ignore them.


Letter from Robert Carter to John Savage, April 13, 1724

-1-

[Corotoman, Lancaster County, Virginia]
Aprill the 13th. 17234
Mr: Jno. Savage -- --

     I have already written you word I Should Employ
Mr. Barber to assist you in my Surveys, this comes with him
I have given him an Accot: of the places acording to Captain Russells
Information, that is the two places upon the broad runn the Lands
upon the Bull runn and its branches up towards the Sugar Land
runn and the Lands upon the Goose Creek from the fork there
of and outwards, Resolve if possible to have my Surveys
made this Spring and next fall, I Expect he will make two
or may be four Surveys before he comes Down, I think
twill be the best way to Send him upon the broard runn and
I Should be very Glad if you will go upon the branches of
the bull runn and likewise upon the head of Goose Creek
this Creek it seems forks into two branches Captain Turbervill
would have had a warrant to Survey the fork of that
Creek but I resolve to give no warrant there about till I am
Served, My Last Letter to you was by Captain Eskridge
who undertook to Send it to Major Smiths from whom he
said you would be Sure of it, I now write to both Russells
who you know have promised me to be your Guides I also
write to the Jermans to be your Chain carriers upon whom
I hope I may Depend, If you think proper to make use
of Mr. Barber in any of the Surveys upon the warrants --
Directed to you he will be Contented to take half the pay
and I am Sure there will be work Enough for you both
during my time, people wait till my warrants &Ca. According to my Directions are laid
off and then there are Abundance that Offer to take up
Lands so that I think in respect to your own Interest a [s]
well as mine You ought to be Glad of the Assistanc [e of]
Mr. Barber and to give him all the Encourage [ment]
that possibly you can --

-2-


     The Division of the Great Tract into two plats and Surveys
I hope you will finish and Send me in a Short time, There is
my warrant for enlarging my Tract called the Red Oak Land
you may do at your leisure Sometime in the Summer I
hope you may find a time for it

     Mr. James Carter tells me that Mr. Scott the
Minister wants his Pattent for the waste Land about
200 Acres joining to a Thousand Acres he bought of John
Waugh upon Woolf Runn that it was Surveyed by Hooper
and Appears in the platt, now I know nothing of all this
Mr. Carter tells me of several other persons want their
pattents, Crosbys Deed is gone and I have never a Survey now
before me that a Deed hath not Issued upon So that I must Send
all these people to you for Satisfaction, As for your Direct
ions about my own Surveys I was so full and perticular
to you when you was here that I dont know how to say more
only to desire you to redouble your diligence and to Treat
Mr. Barber with all kindness and Civility

     Mr. Barber hath copies of several Deeds that lie
upon the bull runn which will be proper to be had by him
that goes there, Mr. Jns: Shelton of Westmoreland made some
Surveys about Ten years ago, Mr. Heath Mr. Morriss Jones
also made Surveys and some others upon the bull runn
and the branches of it but took out no Deeds, these Surveys
I would have you take no notice of and if Sam Russ Lands
or Captain Eskridges lyes in your may you may surround them
if you find it proper it is but making an Exception in the
Deed, Here I have aprehension there may be taken up a
large quantity of very good Land

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter letter book, 1723 June 16-1724 April 23, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.

[1] John Savage was a surveyor, later (1734) to be employed by Lord Fairfax while attempting to establish the boundaries of the proprietary. (Harrison. Landmarks. . . . p. 619.)

[2] Thomas Barber of Richmond County was a surveyor, and would be appointed a justice of that county in 1730, tobacco inspector in 1734, and sheriff in 1736. (McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . . . , 4[1721-1739]: 215, 342, and 369.)

[3] William Russell (1680?-1741) was a well-known ranger andexplorer who eventually settled in Prince William County (later Fauquier). Fairfax Harrison thinks he may have been one of the rangers who accompanied Spotswood's Knights of the Golden Horseshoe. (Harrison, Landmarks of Old Prince William. p. 204.)

[4] Broad Run extends southwest from Lake Manassas in Prince William County and joins Kettle Run just west of present-day Lake Jackson of the Occoquan River, south of the town of Manassas. ( ADC. Regional Northern Virginia. [Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002.] pp. 77, 84, 85, 89.)

[5] Bull Run is a major tributary of the Occoquan River, forming the foundary between today's Prince William and Fairfax counties, and then between Prince William and Loudoun counties.

[6] Sugarland Run flows into the Potomac River in modern Fairfax County along the Loudoun County line.

[7] Goose Creek flows into the Potomac River just east of Leesburg in modern Loudoun County.

[8] Governor Alexander Spotswood had encouraged immigration of Germans into Virginia in 1714, and they settled "in what was then Stafford Co. . . .. later Prince William and now Fauquier." The men worked in Spotswood's iron mines, but around 1718 took grants in the proprietary in what was then Stafford County. (See "Germantown" in Harrison. Landmarks. . . . pp. 207-221. and Elizabeth Chapman Denny Vann and Margaret Collins Denny Dixon. Virginia's First German Colony. Richmond: Privately printed, 1961. p. 33.)

[9] Alexander Scott (d. 1738), had received an M. A. degree from the University of Glasgow before coming to Virginia. He was the minister of Overwharton Parish, Stafford County, and a considerable land speculator. There are numerous references to him in Fairfax Harrison's Landmarks of Old Prince William. (Copeland and MacMaster. The Five George Masons. . . . p. 78; and Harrison. Landmarks. . . . pp. 146, 158, 200, 257, 284-85, 337, 665.)

[10] Wolf Run has not been located. It does not appear: on the Fry-Jefferson map; in ADC. Regional Northern Virginia. (Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002, which covers Fairfax, Fauquier, Loudoun, and Prince William counties); or in Harrison. Landmarks. . . .

[11] The reference to "Jns. Shelton" may be to John Chilton (Chelton) (d. 1744), who was a "son of Mark and Sarah (Keys) Chilton, who described himself as a planter of Prince William County when on 1733 July 17-18 he sold to Richard Blackburn 764 acres. He apparently lived in Westmoreland County earlier because his name is on the lists of justices of that county from 1712 to 1721. ( Jeff Carter. "Chilton Home Page." http://www.geocities.com/Heartland/Acres/2234/chilton/biography/chb012.html; and Norris. Westmoreland County, Virginia. pp. 675-76.)
