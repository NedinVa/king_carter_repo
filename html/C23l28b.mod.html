Letter from Robert Carter to Captain [Thomas] Hooper, December 28, 1723

     Robert Carter writes to Captain [Thomas] Hooper, December 28, 1723, chastising his friend and business associate for his failure to notify him that Captain Charles Broadwater wanted land on Dogue Run. Colonel George Mason and Robert Alexander applied to Carter first and were given warrants which makes it impossible for him to give Broadwater a warrant in that area. He also lets Hooper know that he expects Hooper to write more often, and to pay more attention to making surveys for Carter's own land entries.


Letter from Robert Carter to Captain [Thomas] Hooper, December 28, 1723

-1 -

[Corotoman, Lancaster County, Virginia]

Decr: 28th: 1723

Capt: Hooper

      Yours of the 3d: of December just now came to me Colonel
Mason wrote to me a great whle since and I have passed
a Warrant for the Dogue Land to him which I cannot
recall so that Mr Broadwater must thank You for
his disapointment in keeping his Entry in Your pocket
and never giving me any account of it Yesterday
I passed two Warrants for three Thousand Acres Each
one on the upper side of great Hunting Creek the other
on Pimmots Run between the Lands of Colonel Lee and
Mr. Scot I am afraid this Letter will affect Mr Broad
waters second Entry however I have sent him a Warrant
accordng to his bounds believing that may best please
him should I send him a Warrant for the Land Colonel
Mason has already one for it would be in my estate
but picking his pocket if after Masons four hundred Acres
is laid off there be any Land left Broadwater has a mind
to let him signify it to Me and I will take care to
reserve it for him which is the greatest Kindness
I can now do for him

     You are very short Me thinks in your writing
Sure You have other Subjects enough to entertain me
with. I hear You have been in the upper parts making
some surveys but I do not hear You have Yet had any
regard to My own Entries You will do well to consider
my patience will not hold out much longer and [ ... ] [ . . . ]
You know I will make use of soemd somebody that [ ... ] [will]
do my business if You will not which shall be a[ ... ] [ll at]
present from Your
                             Friend

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter letter book, 1723 June 16-1724 April 23, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.



[1] George Mason III (c. 1690-1735), justice, sheriff, burgess, and county lieutenant of Stafford County, father of the constitutional theorist. (Copeland and MacMaster, The Five George Masons. pp. 50-86 ; and George Harrison Sanford King, The Register of Overwharton Parish Stafford County Virginia 1723-1758 And Sundry Historical and Genealogical Notes . [Fredericksburg, VA: privately printed, 1961.] )

[2] This probably is present Dogue Creek in Fairfax County, lying along the southwestern side of what was once called Mt. Vernon Neck, near Mt. Vernon. "Dogue Creek is a tidal tributary of the Potomac River in Fairfax County, Virginia, named for the Doeg Indians." ( Alexandria Drafting Company. Regional Northern Virginia. [Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002.]. pp. 23, 29; and Wikipedia, http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dogue_Creek. 12/8/2010. )

[3] Charles Broadwater (d. 1733) was described by Fairfax Harrison as "a merchant mariner [who] had been established in the Potomac freshes for a decade when he died in 1733." (Harrison. Landmarks. . . . p. 413. )

[4] "Hunting Creek is a cove and tributary stream of the Potomac River between the City of Alexandria and Fairfax County in Virginia. It is formed by the confluence of Cameron Run and Hoof's Run. The community of Huntington takes its name from the creek. Jones Point forms the north side. Dyke Marsh is just to the south. George Washington Memorial Parkway crosses it on a bridge. The creek is sometimes referred to as Great Hunting Creek, to distinguish it from Little Hunting Creek." It appears on the Fry-Jefferson map of 1751. ( Wikipedia, http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunting_Creek. 12/9/2010 ; and Alexandria Drafting Company. Regional Northern Virginia. [Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002.] pp. 36, 45. )

[5] Pimmits Run is located in today's Fairfax County, and Wikipedia's description of its meandering route is good: [It] "runs from Fairfax County to the Potomac River at Chain Bridge in the Arlingwood neighborhood of Arlington County." There is a neighborhood and a park bearing the Pimmits name along its route. Wikipedia also notes, "The stream was named for John Pimmit who in 1675 was an overseer for William Fitzhugh (1651-1701). John was naturalized a citizen in 1679, and died by drowning in 1688-1689. He was survived by three children." It is also stated that a citation was needed for this information. It almost certainly was taken from Fairfax Harrison's Harrison. Landmarks of Old Prince William, pages 68 and 75. ( Alexandria Drafting Company. Regional Northern Virginia. [Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002.]. pp. 20-21. )

[5] Thomas Lee (1690-1750) of Westmoreland County was the son of Richard Lee II, and nephew of Edmund Jenings; he would build "Stratford," and succeed Carter on the Council. For a good article on Thomas Lee, see that on Stratford plantation's website. ( Burton J. Hendrick. The Lees of Virginia: Biography of a Family. [Boston: Little Brown, 1935]. pp. 48, 51, etc. )
