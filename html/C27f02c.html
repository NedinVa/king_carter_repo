Summary



Letter from Robert Carter to William Robertson, June 2, 1727

     Robert Carter writes to William Robertson, June 2, 1727, clerk of the Council, asking him to review a letter and give "Mr. Cary" advice "in the Duty of his Office" as a friend because Cary "Seems not yet well to understand." He sends Robertson all the news he has received from letters and gazettes just arrived concerning the siege of Gibraltar and concerns in Britain.


Letter from Robert Carter to William Robertson, June 2, 1727

-1 -

Rappahannock [Lancaster County, Virginia]

June 2d: 1727

Mr. Wm: Robertson

Sir --

     The Enclosed will inform you of the business
the Bearer comes upon I have advised him to call upon
you in his way that you may give Mr. Cary your advice
as a friend in the Duty of his Office which as to me he Seems not yet well
to understand The Bearer will show you Mr. Carys Permit
however I have Sent you a Copy of it and in what you Say
to him I hope you will lay a Side all private Views. after you
have perused my Letter I desire you'l Seal it up and deliver
it to the Master.

     A Ship from Bristol arrived here this day
brings to me Several Letters from London of the 15 & 16 of Ma[r] ch
Also Some prints I mean Gazetts to the 4th: of Mar. and a Vote
of the Commons of the 13th: of Mar: The nation is a fire, At the

-2 -


Memorial of Monsieur De Palm the Comons address [illegible]
so do Several of the great bodies of the nation and no doubt addresses
will fly from all parts The royal Burroughs of Scotland have
Address'd The Deputy Leiuts: and the Justices of the Peace for the County
of Middlesex The Prisedent Vice President and the whole
body of Artillary of the City of London The Mayor &c: of Devon
of Leeds, of Warcham in Dorset. of Thetford. of Kingstone up
on Thames, of Lewis in Sussexs as to the Seige of Gibralter there
is nothing new about it The Master who left Bristol the 1st
of Aprill Says there is no manner of fear of the fate of that place
Mr. Bradley Says they are in daily Expectation the warr
will be declared against the Empourer In which he reckons will
Engage most of the powers in Europe will be Engaged on one Side
or the other, Mr. Perry is afraid if the blaze break out if will not
be Easily Extinguished, Mr. Cary of the 22d: of March Says there
is little likelyhood of the Spaniards taking Gibraltar and so hopes
matters will Soon come to an Accommodation for general
peace thus you have the Cheif of my publick news I am


Sir
Yor: most humble Servt:

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter letter book, 1727 May-1728 July, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity.

[1] The Mr. Cary to whom Carter refers may have been Wilson Cary who had been appointed naval officer of the lower district of James River on 1726 April 28, but there were several men of the name at this time. (McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . . . , 4[1721-1739]:99-100. )

[2] Carter's reference has not been identified.

[3] Britain had seized Gibraltar from Spain during a war in 1704, and had been given it permanently by the 1713 Treaty of Utrecht. However, Spain was never happy with the loss, and had threatened, and then laid siege to Gibraltar in March 1727. The effort was unsuccessful and abandoned in June of that year. The treaty of Seville of 1729 ceded Gibraltar to Britain. ( "Gibraltar" http://www.worldstatesmen.org/Gibraltar.html 5/28/2003 )

[3] James Bradley was a London merchant with whom Carter dealt from at least 1723 until his death. As noted in his letter to Bradley of 1727 May 17, Bradley owned the Welcome, but little information about Bradley has been located.
