June 27, 1718
Letter from Robert Carter to Robert Burridge, 1718 June27

     Robert Carter writes to Robert Burridge, a merchant in LymeRegis, Dorset, England, on June 27, 1718, reporting a draft onBurridge to John Parker, and reminding Burridge that he had askedCarter to buy some tobacco for him. Now he has written to Cartercomplaining about the high prices in Virginia for tobacco, and Carternotes "Indeed there hath been no such time produced the like withinmy memory." The Scots merchants are buying up "most ordinary, meaneststuff in the country " at these extraordinary prices. Cartercontinues that he has not had the opportunity of shipping tobacco toBurridge this year but will next if a Lyme ship presents itself. Heasks Burridge to pressure his brother John (a London merchant andmember of Parliament) to account to Carter for 9 hogheads sent him 2years earlier. He concludes that Burridge complains at length aboutDaniel McCarty who, in turn, complains about Burridge. He does notknow where the truth is, but hopes "you may be so much friends toyour own peace to compromise your differences among yourselves," and counsels that theonly ones who gain in lawsuits are the lawyers.


Letter from Robert Carter to RobertBurridge , June 27, 1718

[Corotoman, Lancaster County,Virginia]

June 27th 1718.

To Robert Burridge
Sir,

-1 -

     My last advised you of a draft
I had made on you to John Parker for
£50. In one of your's this year you
advise me topurchase you some Tobo
upon bills. In another you controul
yourself upon the news you hadof the
high prices given here. Indeed there
hath been no such timeproduced
the likewithin my memory. 21,
22, 23, 24 & 25 p Ct hath been
given thisyear for Tobo. The most
ordinary, meanest stuff in the country
hath sold at theseprices and the
Scotch are still laying up all the refuse
and trash they can meet with. what the
misterie of their trade is I don't
know; but sure I am were we to give
these prices for suchgoods we should
never see our principal by a great deal.

     No freight this year within my

-2 -


reach hath offered to your Port .
Youmust not thence conclude
I have done with sending Tobo.
thither. Next year, God willing,
I intend to be at it again upon
the first freight I meet with.

     Your brother John , the Paria=
ment [sic] man, stands in my debt for
nine Hhds of Tobo I sent him two
years ago -- would never yet let me
know what he sold it for. I am
very sorry there issuch a flaw in
the reputation of one of your family.
Methinks you shouldbe so much
concernedat it as to attempt everything
in your power to bring him into the
humour of doing me justice:and
pray interest yourself so much at
least in my behalf to get an accot
what is become of mygoods. I
find 'tis in vain for meto write
to him. You'll do your family but
a credit to bestiryourself so
effectually that I may have my money.

     I find Capt. [Daniel] McCarty and you

-3 -


can by no means set your horses
together. You complainmightily
against him and he is as big with
complaints of you. Who is in the
wrong I can't tell.One story is
always good till another is heard.
I wish you may be so much
friends toyour own peace to
compromise your differences among
yourselves. Few get so much in the
end in going into contentions of
Law, set aside the Lawyers and
Officers. But this is noneof my
business.


I remain, Sir

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Minor-Blackford Papers, James Monroe Law Office and Museum, Fredericksburg,Virginia. These texts are all nineteenth-century copies. Apparentlythere was in existence a letter book of Robert Carter's -- nowlost -- from which the unknown copyist recorded these texts. Asthey are the only texts, the punctuation and "corrections"obviously supplied by the copyist have been retained.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of"Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than"Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especiallyto merchants abroad. The return address, county, and colony have beenadded for clarity to this unheaded letter.


[1] Per 100 pounds

[2] Burridge was located in Lyme Regis, Dorset.

[3] John Burridge (1681?-1753), brother of Robert, wasa merchant in London trading primarily "to Guinea and the West Indies." It is not surprising thatRC could not obtain the sales report and money he mentions in this letter; in 1717, John Burridge'sestate had been seized by the Crown for non-payment of duties on wine. John Burridge represented Lyme inParliament from 1710-1728. (Sedgwick. TheHistory of Parliament . . . Commons. I,509. )
