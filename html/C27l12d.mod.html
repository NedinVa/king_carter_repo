Summary



Letter from Robert Carter to the Earl of Orkney, December 12, 1727

     Robert Carter writes to the non-resident governor of Virginia, the Earl of Orkney, December 12, 1727, thanking him for two letters and for his expression of satisfaction with Carter's administration of the colony while president. He sends two bills of exchange, one for the governor's salary and one for a portion of the fees to which Orkney is entitled, explaining that he will have Commissary James Blair review the accounts on Orkney's behalf. He solicits Orkey's support for his son Charles to succeed his brother Robert as naval officer [of the Rappahannock] when Robert moves to the Potomac. He closes the letter with comments on the selection of a successor of Nathaniel Harrison as deputy auditor of the colony.


Letter from Robert Carter to the Earl of Orkney, December 12, 1727

-1 -

Rappa [hannock, Lancaster County, Virginia]

Decembr 12. 1727

Right Honble:
Earl of Orkney

My Lord

     I have had the honor to receive two letters
from your Lordship of the 18th of May & 31st. of August Acknowledging
the receipt of the bills of Exchange I remitted with very kind expre-
ssions of your favorable opinion of my Administration in the time
of my Presidentship which gives me a great Deal of Satisfaction pleasure

     Herewith I remitt send to your Lordship two bills of
exchange both Drawn on Mr William Dawkins Merchant of London.
one for £ 377:1: -- being your Ldships part of the Salary from the 25th
of April to the 11th of September the Day our Governor published his commission

-2 -


The other bill for £ 233:2:3: completing your Moiety
of the perquisites as far as my Receipts as Your Lordship will see by the state of the en
closed Accot it would be but too troublesome to send yo my respective
vouchers of the Several Officers to justify the truth of this Accot
there are s everal ome County Clerks that have not yet paid me for
Marriage & Ordinary Licences most several of the former will be paid in tobacco
wch at this time is a poor Comodity when I have received what is behind I intend to get Mr. Comissary to
take an inspection of all the vouchers Accots who from thence will be
able to make it the justice of my proceedings Clear to Your Ldship & I believe will be a method
[illegible] that will give your Lordship full satisfaction

     fif [t] een -- 15 percent is our common Exchange between bills
& Cash I have twenty perCEnt this Year allowed for my bills
the Corn I could not Dispose of for money only a few barrels of it have
been forced to take it to myself & have Allowed A larger price than I have
sold for*

     I must not take upon me to measure the Character of
our Governor but thus far we promise ourselves a great deal of
happiness under his Administration Government

     My Lord in the time of my Administration
happened the Death of Mr Robinson our Naval Officer my Second
Son Robert being of age & married & living most Convenient for the place
I put him into the Naval Office & our Gove [r] nor hath been so kind to rene
w the Comission to him & am in hopes the behaviour of my son will be
So Agreeable as not to deserve a remove he lives with me at present
& how may Years more he may I do not Yet know but the patrimony
I intend for him lies upon Potomack river whenever he removes to it
I should be glad to procure his place for my third son Charles at Mans Estate also
whose living will be on this river I know a word from Your Lordship
to our Governor would be of great weight towards this change if your
Lordship will please to Vouchsafe me such a favour; not but that I
have met with all the kind & Civil distinguishing treatment from our governor Since
his Arrival that I could desire & may perhaps if I live continue
so much in his good Graces to incline him to such a Step upon

-3 -


my own intercession however your Lordship s influence to be sure
will very much facilitate the matter I have a very Large family
to provide for am grown a very Crasy man I have no merit to plead
only that I have served the Government in the station I am in above
illegible Eight seven & twenty Years almost Eight & twenty Years & my recei
vings from it have not answer'd my Expenses

      It will be no news to acquaint your Lordship with the sudden
death of Colonel Harrison one of our Council as well as Deputy
Auditor his place in the gift of Mr Horace Walpole who can
make the most interest with him Its likely will stand the fairest
Chance I shall must remain a Spectator only with wishing that
merit may take place & that since [illegible] this place hath been generally
held by one of our Council who are the judges of our General Court
that for the good of the Country it may fall to the lot of a person of Gravity Experience & some
knowledge in Our laws & constitution for I may presume to
say such a person will not be unuseful to Supply the place
of those that are lately gone off when you [r] shall think it proper Lordship's leisure will
allow me the favour of your remembrance I shall always grate
fully receive it who am who am with all
Dutiful respect


My Lord
your Lordship's
most obliged
most Obedient
& most humble servant

per Christian

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter letter book, 1727 May-1728 July, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity.

The last paragraph of the letter was drafted as an insertion headed "A clause to be added to my Ld Orkneys letter" and it has been placed as intended.

[1] A moiety is one of two approximately equal portions.

[2] Although the text for the following paragraph of the draft is contiguous at this point, the clerk inserted the asterisk which must have been a signal to begin a new paragraph as the subject changes completely.

[3] Carter refers to Christopher Robinson who had been the naval officer of the Rappahannock River and who had died prior to a Council meeting of March 1, 1727. (McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . . . , 4[1721-1739]:128. )

[4] Charles Carter (1707-1764) was Robert Carter's tenth child; his mother was Carter's second wife, Elizabeth (Landon) Willis Carter. Charles would live at "Cleve," King George County, and inherit a number of properties in that area from his father. (Carleton. A Genealogy. . . of Robert Carter. . . . p. 340; and Morton. Robert Robert Carter of Nomini Hall. Numerous references.)

[5] Horace Walpole was the brother of Robert Walpole, the chief minister of England. He held the posts of "auditor general and surveyor general of the royal revenue in the colonies." (Billings. et al. Colonial Virginia: A History. )

[6] James Christian was captain of the Rose, a vessel owned by merchant John Pemberton of Liverpool. (See Carter to Pemberton, 1730 April 15.)
