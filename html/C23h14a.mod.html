James Butler's Statement Concerning the Brent Town Lands, August 14, 1723

     Robert Carter had his clerk record in a letter book a statement by James Butler, August 14, 1723, taken by John Copedge at the Stafford County court concerning the location of the corner of the Brent Town grant in the Northern Neck proprietary.


James Butler's Statement Concerning the Brent Town Lands, August 14, 1723

-1 -

Stafford County [Virginia]

Augst. 14th. Anno 1723.


     A Memorandum taken at Stafford
Court of Mr. James Butler Vizt. That about 38 Years past
he was Waiting Man to Captain [George] Brent, and being with his
Said Master and his Company on the Point where Cedar
Run & Broad Run meets, and Captain Brent took a
tomahawk & marked a White Oak on the Point Standing
On a rock and bid them take notice that from thence he
Would have a Tract of Land of 20. or 30 thousand Acres
And That Tree Should be the beginning & Accordingly
the Year following Came in the Brent Town Grant at
first called the New Town, upon this Information I told
Butler I heard the Corner Tree was down he answered
that if the Corner Tree was down all the Trees was
not down for he marked several about it at the same time
Taken per me -- the Date above -- John Copedge

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter letter book, 1723 June 16-1724 April 23, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.


[1] "Vizt." is the abbreviation for the Latin word "videlicet"; it means "namely."

[2] George Brent (d. 1699) a "surveyor and lawyer," of "Woodstock," Stafford County, was the "resident partner in Virginia" for the development of the Brent Town grant by Nicholas Hayward. (Harrison. Landmarks. . . . p. 178 ff. )

[3] Cedar Run wanders through today's counties of Fauquier and Prince William along the northern border of the Quantico Marine Base joining the Broad Run to form the Occoquan River just southwest of the community of Lake Jackson in Prince William County. In Carter's time, the area was Stafford County. ( Alexandria Drafting Company. Regional Northern Virginia. [Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002.] )

[4] Broad Run extends southwest from Lake Manassas in Prince William County and joins Kettle Run just west of present-day Lake Jackson of the Occoquan River, south of the town of Manassas. ( Alexandria Drafting Company. Regional Northern Virginia. [Alexandria, VA: Alexandria Drafting Company, 2002.] pp. 77, 84, 85, 89. )

[5] John Copedge appears as a justice of the peace in Northumberland County in 1714, but was not listed in 1726 there when the name appears as the surveyor of Lancaster County. ( Louis des Cognets, Jr., English Duplicates of Lost Virginia Records. Baltimore: Genealogical Publishing Co., Inc. 1981, 27, 36. )
