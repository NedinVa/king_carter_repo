Summary



Letter from Robert Carter to John Stark, May 30 and July 9, 1728

     Robert Carter writes to Glasgow merchant John Stark, May 30, 1728, to report the arrival of goods and accounts, and to complain of certain of Stark's accounting methods. He informs Stark of several bills of exchange, and then seeks the merchant's support of the colony's effort to obtain repeal of the act prohibiting the importation of stemmed tobacco into Britain. In a lengthy post script dated July 9th, Carter encloses bill s of lading, complains of Stark's holding his tobacco too long before selling it, relates a misunderstanding over a "hamper" of claret, and orders some burgundy and champagne.


Letter from Robert Carter to John Stark, May 30 and July 9, 1728

-1-

Rappahannock, [Lancaster County, Virginia]

May the 30th: 1728

Mr. John Stark

Sir --

     I have rec'd Sevl. Letters from you this year, the
goods you Sent by the Mazareen have bin landed long ago, I have
bin so often and so long from home, I have not Overhalld them yet
Tother day a pack of goods was put aShoar at my house by Capt: Dun
lop he Says he had them out of Gamell in the Bay, I Suppose these
goods must come from you I have not Seen the inside In
makeing up yor. Accots: of Sales you take a method that to me Seems
very Irronious. First you pay down the whole duty and then you
Sell the greatest part for Export giving Credit for the drawback
upon wch you raise a Commission so that here I have no illegible
to and without any so that here I have no Advantage illegible of my money nay a loss this
is a way of making up an Accot. I have not bin in used to either
in your Port nor any other, when they Sell their Tobbo: for Export
they dont trouble the Accot. with the duty. another thing I observe
[you] dont allow me the full discounts for the Tobbo: Sold for the home Con
sumption I cannot think but I had money in yor. hands to
Entitle me to all those discots. other men when they make up
their Accots: State the Several dutys in different A [rtic] les and allow a duty of 20 perCt. wh [ich] the Actt [...] gives for prompt payment, I desire you
will give me Satisfaction in these things [I have] dis [coursed]

-2-


Capt: Bowman about them. but he cannot give Answer me Satis
faction I have now aboard of Bowman the Lucia I reckon about
24 hogsheads of Tobbo: how many more I Shall put into him I a [m]
not certain yet,

     Herein Send you the following bills of Exchange to wit
Alexr. Scott on yor. Self for £12"-" Robt. Alexander on Neal Buch
annan £11:10"8. Sam. Bowman on yor. Self for £15:18
which desire Credit for, making in the whole £39"8"8" --

     I have drawn on you Payable to Richd. Meeks
for £21"9"8, which I desire you Please to anwer at time,

     Now allow me to turn my head a little on
Politicks. Wee are in a great design to get the Clause in the Act
in the Prohibitting the Steming Tobbo: repeal'd Our Councll &
Burgesses have Addressd his Majesty and made Application to the
Parliamt. about it. Wee have Employd a Gent of our Country
one of our Eminentest Lawyers as our Agent who is going home
to Solicite this Affair, Wee have great reson to hope the London
Merchts. and Outports will Joyn their Interest with us and wee
are told from many of your Countrymen here Yor. represen
tatives will Joyn their helping hand, And that you are generally
hearty aweary of this restraint most Certain it is that by de=
nying us the liberty of Steming our Tobbo: all the Trash and under
Tobbo: is Shipt of. It is become the Common trade thro the Country
to buy up this under Tobbo: as it is calld but more properly Lugs
at half price and none is bad but the Planters can get Something
for it and when they lay out their Crops many will force the
Merchts. to take two or three under hogsheads or Else they will not let
them have their best These things your Merchts. can give you
a better Accot. of then I. Whereas if we are allowd to Stemm
our under Tobbo: we Should Seperate the good and heave the Trash
away and this Stemd Tobbo: would be as valueable nay more
then the best leaf which wee know from long Experience. And wee
cannot think it would hurt the King in his Customs. Stemd

-3-


Tobbo: must be Comsumd in the nation and pay the duty. but this
rubbish Stuff as it is now managed must be either Clandestinly
run or Else Sent abroad either way the King has no Custom, And
the Market prodigious depress'd as well to the damage of the Fare
trader as well as planter, And What Should hinder if the King
is no looser and if the Commodity would be much betterd to the
Merchtsas wee think is very demonstrable but that we may
reasonably promise our Selves Success inthis undertaking.
You make a Considerable figure in Glasgow where the greatest
part of the Tobbo: in North Brittain centures, I thought it not
amiss to give you this Short Entertainmt. abt. this affair
I could name you Several of your Merchts. here that tells us
you will readily and Streneuously add your Endeavours to
take of this prohibition, I am


Sir --
Yor. very humble Servt.

per Denton
Bowman


-4-


                             To be Added to Mr. Starks Letter of the 30th: May

                                                            Rappahannock July 9th: 1728

      The above is a Copy This Accompanys Capt: Bowman
and brings you a bill of Lading for 29 hogsheads of Tobbo: another is ready
for him but he cannot take it in, I have a pretty deal of Tobbo: on
my hands yet to come home and prize where I Shall get it freited
I dont yet know beleive I Shall Choose rather Glasgow then any other Port I
have often Complaind to you of these lagg Sales and upon very good
reason for they always prove the worst now you Say if I would
have Quick returns I must give orders to you to Sell at the Mast, this
will Entirely defeat me of any profit for my money. I am for tak=
ing that way that will return me the most but that I must leave
to your Judgemt. who up are upon the Spot and yet after all I must press
you to make up your Accots: as quick as you can that I may know
my doom Your have bin very larg in making up yor. Accot: of the
James river Tobbo: there is some more coming to you from thence
both of my own and Mr. Burwells Concern tis your mistake to call
it Maddam Burwells the most of this is Tobbo: which wee call
under Tobbo: Therefore if you dispose of it at the Mast it may perhaps
be the best way Your Country men and the Outport Merchants
have bought up this Tobbo: all over Virginia and have given 2d
a pound in their Stores for it I will hope this Tobbo: will rise pretty
well and return us a tolerable price,

     Capt: Gamell Sent ashoar my Pack of Goods
in another Ship was bound hither Dunlop Master and with it
a hamper of Clarret I refused to take it telling him it did not
belong to me I know nothing of it he assured me Gamell told him
it was for me, upon which I was free with it proved good wine

-5-


and very Opportunely come for our Govr. Soon after made me a
Vissit which cost me a good deal of it Since I have understood by
Mr. Gay the wine belongd to Colonel Epps of James river, I Suppose it was
of yor. Shipping all that I can Say is you must Charged my Accot
with it and give Epps Credit or Send him the wine again as you
See fit,

     The Burgundy & Champaign you Sent me from
Dublin Some years ago hath kept so well that I think it is as
good now as when it first came I desire you will Send me 4 doz
of each Sort if you have Opportunity and from the Same person
you bought that by whose Extraordinary care in the well bottling
Corking and the well packing I reckon hath bin a great means
to keep it so well I would also have you Send me 6 or 8 doz: of
right good Clarret I Shall Conclude at present

herein is a bill of Lading for 12 hhd belonging to Colonel Page

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter letter book, 1727 May-1728 July, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia. The letter book has suffered some damage to the first page of the letter which is faded along its margins, and has some textual damage.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity.

[1] John Stark was a merchant, probably the one to whom Carter referred when he wrote to Micajah Perry on 1723 July 4 that he had drawn an order on "Mr. Stark of Glasgow." On that same day, he wrote to a John Stark, referring to Captain Bowman.

[2] Carter noted in his diary 1727 January 19 the arrival of the Mazareen at his house and a visit from her captain, Richard Kelsick, four days earlier.

[3] The Martha was commanded by a Captain Dunlop; see Carter to Richard Oswald & Company 1729 August 11.

[4] Captain Samuel Bowman commanded the Lucia. Carter mentioned this vessel in his diary in June 1724, and again on 1726 March 4 when he wrote that she "came in had 20 Weeks Passage."

[5] Parliament had passed the act forbidding the importation of stemmed tobacco in 1722. Randolph would not leave for England until 1729; his mission would be successful. ( Arthur Pierce Middleton. Tobacco Coast: A Maritime History of the Chesapeake Bay in the Colonial Era. [Newport News, VA: Mariners' Museum, 1953.] p. 116.)

[6] The John & Betty was a Liverpool ship often carrying slaves owned by merchant John Pemberton. In 1726 the captain was John Gale, and in the next year, she was commanded by a Captain William Denton. The ship would be lost in 1729. (Wright. Letters of Robert Carter. . . . p. 18, n. 23; Carter to Pemberton, 1730 April 15; and Carter to William Dawkins, June 28, July 26, and August 22, 1727, for Denton's first name.)

[7] Captain Gamell has not been identified.

[8] The Martha was commanded by a Captain Dunlop; see Carter to Richard Oswald & Company 1729 August 11.
