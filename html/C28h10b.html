Letter from Robert Carter to William Dawkins, August 10, 1728

     Robert Carter writes to London merchant William Dawkins, August 10, 1728, to cover a bill of lading, report the arrival of accounts Dawkins had sent on the Sarah, and comment on the sales. He complains about York River tobacco receiving higher prices than his. He sends a bill of exchange and comments on his efforts to collect debts due the merchant, and on charges Dawkins has made on the account of the Nathaniel Burwell estate.


Letter from Robert Carter to William Dawkins, August 10, 1728

-1-

Rappahannock, [Lancaster County, Virginia]

Augst: 10th: 1728

Mr: Wm: Dawkins

Sir --

     This concludes me for this time herein Send
you a bill of Lading for 45 hogsheads of my own Crop Tobbo: I cannot praise
it from my own Eye Sight, but the persons your under whose Inspecting Care it was made
to whom I give large Salarys for that very purpose assure me it is very
good and well handled,

     Yor. packets by the Sarah Via Maryland came
to my hands two days ago bringing me the Sales of Mr. Burwells Tobbo:
wth. an Accot: Currt: of that Estate, and an Accot: of Sales of 20 of mine
by Keeling you Say that you had about 25 left which you were daily get
ing of and would send the Accot. as Soon as finishd I will rest in hopes
those unaccounted for will come out as well as the Bettys it will ever be
a mistery to me that our Tobbo: made here falls so much Short in
Price to the York river Tobbo: I beleive I may appeal to yor. memory
to Say it was not so formerly Sure I am neither their Grounds nor
their care exceed ours, when you have closed the Sales I hope you will
take the first Opportunity to Send me an Accot. Currt.

     Now Send you Capt Doves Exchange on your Self
for £45"18. and mine and D, Lee's for £7"6"8 I have rec'd a ballance
that belongs to you from Tom Winter of One pound One Shilling
and Six pense to be charged to my Accot. I have hopes in the long run
to get your Outstanding Debt of Hutchins's Execrs: his widdw: promises
me fairly I will prevail with her if I can to get her bond Conollys
is a Special poor fellow but I beleive he is honest and will pay wt.
he is able, and before you cant Expect it,

     Capt:: Dove brought me in Some odd things and
I thought I had given orders to you to let him have the money but
Dove tells me Mr Athawes could find no Such order I observe you have
charged half perCt: upon the Sale of Mr. Burwells Tobbo: for which you
have no Allowance from me I shall discourse Colonel Page who act [s]
with me in that affair and you will hear futher from us about
it, I am



Sir, Yor. very humble Servt.

per the Carter
Copy per the Capt.Wilcoxs

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Letter book, 1728 August-1731 July, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity.

[1] Captain [James?] Richardson commanded the Sarah and was based in Weymouth.

[2] Captain William Keiling commanded the Betty. ( Survey Report 6800 summarizing Adm. 68/194, Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.)

[3] This may be John Hutchins of Lancaster County who died in 1727. (Sorrells. title>Landholders & Landholdings. p.30. )

[4] Patrick Connelly appears on a 1716 list of titheables in Christ Church Parish, Lancaster County ( "Tithables in Lancaster Co., 1716." William and Mary Quarterly 1st. ser., 21[July 1912]: 107.)
