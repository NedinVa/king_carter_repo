Summary



Letter from Robert Carter to Edward Tucker, May 25 and July 9, 1728

     Robert Carter writes to Weymouth merchant Edward Tucker, May 25, 1728, to report the arrival of Captain Willson after a long voyage via Antigua, several bills of exchange, Captain Brett's inability to take his tobacco, and his hope that Willson will be able to carry it. The bulk of the letter is devoted to persuading Tucker, a member of the House of Commons, to support the colony's desire to repeal the act of Parliament probhibiting the importation of stemmed tobacco into England. In a post script dated July 8th, Carter notes that he plans to send 30 hogsheads of tobacco in Captain Willson's ship, reports bills of exchange, and orders beer, malt, and ale.


Letter from Robert Carter to Edward Tucker, May 25 and July 9, 1728

-1-

Rappahannock, [Lancaster County, Virginia]

May the 25th: 1728

Edward Tucker Esqr.

Sir --

     After a round about Voyage by the way of
Antego Capt: Willson came Safe in at last, I had Some damage
in my Beer Necessity forced him in his long passage to refresh
his men and himself with Some of it; but that he and I shall
Accommodate,

     Herein Send you a bill of Exchange of Thos: Edwards
for £13:12:3:d Also John Kirks for £8"9:11 both on your Self
which desire Credit for,

     Capt: Brett soon after his coming in Spoke to me
for Some freight, Accordingly I promised him, but the number of
hhds: I could not then tell, I was a long time kept from home at
our Assembly and General Court, he sent two Sloops into our
parts, the last was after I got home and might have taken in
Some of my Tobbo: but he had Some better freinds to pleasure
I Suppose and so left me totally out Capt: Willson I intend to
ship upon if he doth not Serve me as Brett hath done he hath
Sent me word he will be with me in a few days when we Shall
Adjust that matter,

     It is the general Opinion of the Gent in the Country
that the great Occasion of sending of so much Trash and under
Tobbo: is by the restraint wee are under from Steming for now all
the ground leaves and under Tobbo: are tyed up and go to market
none is so bad but the Outports Merchts. and the North Brittains
will buy at Some Price or other which wee reckon Computing
through the Country amounts to Several thousand hogsheads and does
very much depress the market for the better Sort of Tobbo: Whereas
if the good were pickt out by Steming it would be as Valueable
as the best Tobbo: wee make. Wee think so much of the Trash of our
Tobbo: going home is no advantage to the King in his Customs

-2-


and greatly to the damage of Mercht as well as the Planter in depress
ing the Price of all Tobbo: for we Conclude this under Scruby under
Tobbo: is either Clandestinely run or Serves the Export trade either
way no Custom is paid for it. The Country in general is so deep
ly Senseable of this great Evill that the Councill and Burgesses
have address'd the King and made Application to the Parliamt
for the repealing of the Clause in the Act of Parliamt. prohibit
ing Steming, And we have Employd Mr. John Randolph one
of our Eminentest Lawyers whose is [sic] now going home to Solic
ite this affair; wee are made to hope that the Lond: Merchts.
& the Outports will Joyn their utmost Strength with us to get
it Effected, And we are promised by the North Brittains among
us that their representatives will give their helping hand,
And if all this Interest go along with us wee have Strong
hopes wee cannot fail of Success, Our Agent is instructed
with our reasons to the Parliamt. to prevail with them in
granting our Petition. You have the honour now to be a Member
of the house of Commons and in that respect a proper Person to be
applyd too for your Assistance Although I believe you are
already so well Acquainted with the great mischiefs wee
lye under by this gravious restraint which that you will want no
Arguments to Induce you to be hearty in this design I Ex=
pect it will be writ you by most of the Gent you Correspond wth.
upon this Topick I am per WoodwardCopy per Trice --


Sir --
Yor. most humble Servt:

per Woodward
Copy per Trice --


-3-


               To be Added to Mr. Tuckers Letter the 25th May

Sir --                                                             Rappahannock July 9th: 1728

     The Above is a Copy Capt: Willson is now coming into
the Indian Creek and as soon as he hath taken in a little Tobbo: there
he comes away to our river for the rest of his Load am afraid he will
hardly dispatch time Enough to get away with this Fleet I shall
have about 30 hogsheads in him,

     Herein are three first bills of Exchange drawn upon
upon [sic] your Self Richard Neal for £2"-"-Peter Hack for £3"-"-
William Jones for £2 which I desire Credit for, I have had ill luck
with my Beer for these two years however I would desire you to
Send me 2 hogsheads of your best Dorchester Beer, well and Carefully
bottled of and packt up Also two hogsheads of your best Malt I hope it
will be Cheaper then the last and if your Southham be right good
this year I would have a hogshead of it put into bottles with care I
will have no more of it come in hogsheads By Willson I Shall be more
Particular remain at presentSir
Yor. most humble Servt.
per Trice --
per Willson

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter Letter Book, 1727 April 13-1728 July 23, Carter Family Papers, Virginia Historical Society, Richmond. The word "Mr." has been struck before Tucker's name in the salutation, but the software will not allow it to be shown struck through as it will in the body of the text.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity to the heading on the draft.

[1] No information about Captain Willson has been located.

[2] "Thos. Edwards, a little petty Fogging Lawyer the Clark of ourCounty that hath as much Mettle and more cunning for Contention then his predecessor had" Carter wroteto Landon Jones, July 23, 1723. His opinion of Edwards later changed for there are more appreciate mentions of him in Carter's diary. Edwards was clerkof the Lancaster County court from 1720-1746. ( Within the Court House at Lancaster. Lively, VA: Lively Printing Services, Lively, VA: Lively Printing Services, [1976]. p. 15.)

[3] John Brett was master of Weymouth merchant Edward Tucker's ship, the Princess Amelia, which had been commanded by a Captain Lawrence until he was drowned. (Survey Report 9729 detailing the Weymouth Port Books, found in the microfilms of the Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.)

[4] Parliament had passed the act forbidding the importation of stemmed tobacco in 1722. Randolph would not leave for England until 1729; his mission would be successful. ( Arthur Pierce Middleton. Tobacco Coast: A Maritime History of the Chesapeake Bay in the Colonial Era. [Newport News, VA: Mariners' Museum, 1953.] p. 116.)

[5] Thomas Woodward commanded the Providence, a ship owned by Captain John Hyde & Company, during a number of voyages to the colony, 1723-1727. ( Survey Report 6800 summarizing Adm. 68/194 and Survey Report 6801 summarizing Adm 68/195, Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia, and Carter's letter to the firm, 1723 September 17.)

[6] The 140 ton Welcome was owned by London merchant James Bradley to whom Carter would write about her on 1727 May 17. John Trice (Frice) was her captain, 1723-1728. ( Survey Report 6801 summarizing Adm 68/195, 154r, found in the microfilms of the Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia.)

[7] Carter may refer to the Indian Creek that is the boundary between Lancaster and Northumberland counties. "It lies between Bluff Point Neck and Fleets Bay Neck, flowing about 3.6 miles into Fleets Bay. . . . " (Miller. Place-Names . . . . p. 72.)
