Letter from Robert Carter to Samuel Smith, January 22, 1728

     Robert Carter writes to chide Samuel Smith of King and Queen County, January 22, 1728, for not making the required legal arrangements for Smith's brigantine, the Mary to unload its cargo of rum and take on tobacco for its voyage back to England. Carter notes he is willing to post the required bond provided Smith will sign a counter bond to indemnify Carter.


Letter from Robert Carter to Samuel Smith, January 22, 1728

Coro[toma]n: [Lancaster County, Virginia]

Janry 22d: 1727/8

Mr. Samuel Smith

Sir --

     Capt Price in your Brigantine got
into my Creek this afternoon the disasters she met with in
the Bay from the late storm and what materials will
be wanting to refit her I shall leave to the relation of
your men and the Capt who writes to you at large

     Her stay hath been so long that I be=
gan to grow out of Expectation of seeing her upon
my proposals her burthen being so much larger than
my information and the damage she hath now sustained

ver if Mr Burgess and some others that I have expecta
tions from will do anything considerable, I beleive we shall
be able to give her a tolerable good dispatch you ha

      You have been very wrong in not sending
a permit from Your Off[ice]rs of the goods you have removed
in her and a certificate of the Legal importation of
the rum and of the payment of the Duty there and without
such certificate no Officer here can permit the landing
of it without Obliging the payment of the Duty over ag=
ain and without Obliging the present Master to an oath
to the contents of his Cargo and that the Vessel was Lega=
lly entered in your district or else must enter her afresh.
you know all these things better than I and that it is
the plain direction of the law indeed I think Mr Cary
was too scrupilous in not giving you a certificate upon
your oath made to the goods before Majr. Newton one of your Justices and
you have not so much as sent up the oath you took
before him my son is not at home but I have thought
the shortest way for you would be for your M[ast]er to
give bond for payment of the Duty over again and
leave you to releive your self by a Certificate from your
Off[ice]rs hereafter but you will think of this and give all di
rections according to your own thoughts

     Another thing is wanting the Off[ice]rs here
cannot permit your Vessel to take in tobbo until bond
is given to them in a thousd or two thousd pounds accor
ding to her burthen for her unlaiding her tobbo in Britta
in being an enumerated commodity your letter for this en
gaging your self to be bound with your masters security in the

proper sum for his legal delivery according to the act
of parliament and returning Certificate thereof with=
in the limited time I shall will be a foundation to me for me Adventure to by signing the bond
in your stead upon your Undertaking to give a counter bond
to indemnify me this must be done before your Vess=
el can proceed in taking in tobbo and this I dare say
you are no stranger to I am


Sir
Your most humble Servt. --

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter Letter Book, 1727 April 13-1728 July 23, Carter Family Papers, Virginia Historical Society, Richmond. Carter review the clerk'd draft of this letter and added an occasional word in his hand as is indicated by the use of italics.

The county and colony have been added for clarity to the heading on the draft.

[1] Samuel Smith was a resident of King and Queen County where he was a justice, and would be sheriff in 1730 and 1731.(McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . . . , 4[1721-1739]:215, 235. )

[2] Robert Carter's home, "Corotoman," was located on the bank of the Rapphannock River in Lancaster County close to the present-day community of Weems. A creek known today as Carter's Creek ran inland near the house.
