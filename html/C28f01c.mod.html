Letter from Robert Carter to John Pemberton and Company, June 1, 1728

     Robert Carter writes to Liverpool merchant John Pemberton and Company, June 1, 1728, about the shipment of tobacco belonging to the firm and the arrangements he has made, and reporting several bills of exchange.


Letter from Robert Carter to John Pemberton and Company, June 1, 1728

-1 -

Rappahannock, [Lancaster County, Virginia]

June the 1st: 1728

Mr. John Pemberton & Company
Freirs: of the Ship Rose

Gent --

      This Encloses to you a bill of lading
for 35 hogsheads of Tobacco on board the John and Betty under the
[tobacco mark] 's mark, The weights whereof and of the 33 hogsheads by the rose
with the 30 Shipped on the Mary [illegible] are likewise herein Sent Together
with a Stated Account of the money Concern as far as it is gone
I have drawn upon You to Captain Denton for the Impost of
these 35 hogsheads £3"11" -- to be placed to the Sales of the Tobacco

     On the 10th of April I gave notes to Captain
Brackhill for 30 add [sic ] hogsheads of your Tobacco lying in Potomac
upon his promises of Send ing for them [illegible] ing a Short time after, My
Occasions Soon Called me from home he meeting with Tobacco
for his business a little more Convenient never Sends for yo rs : keeps
my note until the 8th. of May and then returned it leting me know
he Should not Send for the Tobacco This Story Capt. Denton very well knows -- having no prospect of Shipping it
for your Port have Sent for it round in a Sloop of my own Intend
ing to Ship it in the Carter for London believing you will rather
have it any where than to let it lie in the Country all the year
and having often heard Mr. Pemberton when he could not get
his Tobacco to his own Port choose to have it to London before any
other place, When it is all got together I reckon there will be near

-2 -


about 40 hogsheads , I shall take care to Consign this Tobacco to Mr.
Pemberton that he may choose his own Merchant in London for
the Sale of it,

     It will not be amiss to observe to you that although
Captain Denton has Signed the bills of Lading for 35 hogsheads of this
mark he Says both his Mate and Boatman assures him there
are so many I am very doubtful there is but 34 for Certain
I am 3 of the lightest hogsheads were Sent ashore to my Prize house
and were there put into 2

     The day of payment for Awbrys Debt is already
past he lives 150 Miles from me have heard nothing
from him hope I shall be able to recurit you a part at least
if not the whole of his money by t some of the latest Ships

     herewith I Send you the following bills of
Exchange to wit, George Eskridge on Mr. Pemberton for £70"-"-
Hugh Brent on Edward Tucker Esquire Weymouth for £34"-"-
Robert Mitchell on Bryan Blundell for £65"-"-£169 in
in [sic ] the whole and have given directions to Mr. Pember
ton to pay you £18 Dr. Edgars Debt You will take notice
that the receiving the Tobacco yet unshipped and the Nails for the
lining is to be charged hereafter This I think I have given
you as full an Account of your Concern as I am at present able



Remain
Gent
Your very humble Servant --

per Denton
herein is the Mark Numbers and
weights of the 29 hogsheads went by
the Rose --
per Denton
per Brackhill Trice

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Robert Carter Letter Book, 1727 April 13-1728 July 23, Carter Family Papers, Virginia Historical Society, Richmond.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity to the heading on the draft.

[1] The John & Betty was a Liverpool ship owned by merchant John Pemberton; she often carryied slaves into the colony. In 1726 the captain was John Gale, and in the next year, she was commanded by a Captain William Denton. The ship would be lost in 1729. (Wright. Letters of Robert Carter. . . . p. 18, n. 23 ; Carter to Pemberton, 1730 April 15; and Carter to William Dawkins, June 28, July 26, and August 22, 1727, for Denton's first name.)

[2] James Christian was captain of the Rose, a vessel owned by merchant John Pemberton of Liverpool. (See Carter to Pemberton, 1730 April 15.)

[3] The Mary was a London ship of 130 tons commanded by James Hopkins, and owned by merchant Robert Cary. ( Admiralty 68/194,ff. 82r, abstracted in Survey Report 6801, Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia. )

[4] The impost was the duty imposed by Britain on imported tobacco, and the cocket (for which a fee was charged) was the certified document issued that the impost had been paid.

[5] There were several vessels with the name Rappahannock . One was based in Liverpool and was commanded by a Captain Francis Etheridge in 1725; she was a 90 ton ship with 13 men. Another of which Charles Whate was captain was of 60 tons with 11 men in 1726 and 1727. Carter's letters mention a Captain Brackhill in 1728, and Captain Loxum in 1733. ( Adm. 68/194, ff.30, found in the microfilms of the Virginia Colonial Records Project, Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia. )

[6] Frank Awbrey (1690?-1741) was an active land speculator in the area that became Loudoun County, and was one of the first justices when Prince William County was organized in 1731. He was sheriff of that county in 1739. (McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . . . , 4[1721-1739]:239, 439; and Harrison. Landmarks. . . . pp. 148, 150, 153-54 ff. )

[7] Hugh Brent was a resident of Christ Church parish in the 1716 survey of tithables of Lancaster County, and he would be sheriff in 1734. ( "Tithables in Lancaster Co., 1716." William and Mary Quarterly 1st. ser., 21[July 1912]: 106-11, and McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . . . , 4[1721-1739]:319. )
