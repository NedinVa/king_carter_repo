Letter from Robert Carter to John King, February 14,1721

     Robert Carter writes to Bristol merchant, John King, February 14,1721, concerning goods that he has received on Captain Sweet's ship.He likes the cider very much, but chides King because the (slave)shoes he has sent are terrible. He orders new ones that are not tobe black because black shoes rot very quickly. He promises to sendsome tobacco to King but complains that Bristol prices are low andfreight rates high, and he reports a bill of exchange he has given toCharles Burgess.


Letter from Robert Carter to John King,February 14, 1721

-1-

Rappahannock, [LancasterCounty, Virginia]

February 14th.1720/21

Mr. John King

Sir --

     This is only a Short line to let You know I have
receivedYour Goods by Captain Sweets Ship, The cider pleases me
well enough, Your Shoos the worst that Ever Ihad from Your
City I pray You to take the First opportunity toSend me 6 dozen
more of the best plainsYou can buy, two dozen of the 16s.
2 dozen . of the 14s. and 2 doz of the 12s. and let them not be
madeof black leather which all these are, they always
prove rotten Iused to have the best shoes from Bristol
of any port in Engd.

     Your market is very low and Freightsrun high
there is butlittle encouragement to Ship anyTobacco to Your
parts however I intend You Some If I can procure a

-2-


reasonable Freight,

     I drew on You in September for£15"14" -- to Charles Burgs.
which I desire You to pay, am Sorry my Tobacco in Darracot Stuck so
long in hand, my hopes were Its going so Early It would
have come to an Early market. Ihave not time to Say
anything more but that I am


Your humble Servant

Your cider is notpleasant
& yet Itpleases me very well.

Per The Tayloe Galley Bristol
Captn. JohnHeard

NOTES



Source copyconsulted: Robert Carter Letter Book, 1720 July-1721 July,BR 227, Huntington Library, Art Collections, and Botanical Gardens,San Marino, California. Printed: Wright. Letters of Robert Carter. . . . pp. 75-76.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of"Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than"Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especiallyto merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added forclarity.

[1] Charles Burgess (d. 1732) was a planter ofLancaster County and a member of the House of Burgesses in 1728 andlater. (Torrance. Virginia Wills andAdministrations,1632-1800. p. 62.)

[2] The Tayloe was wrecked onthe North Carolina coast in December 1726 and the mate and othersaccused of murdering Captain Heard. The Council concluded there wasinsufficient evidence to prosecute them. (McIlwaine. Executive Journals of the Council. . .. , 4(1721-1739): 126-27.)
