Summary



Letter from Robert Carter to James Bradley, August 26, 1729

     Robert Carter writes to London merchant James Bradley, August 26, 1729, to order additional goods including a "close stool," "Mill brasses," nails, stockings, etc., and a suit and accessories for himself. He writes of the heavy rains during the spring that have damaged the crops. In a post script, he asks Bradley to be one of the securities for his so Charles as naval officer of the Rappahannock.


Letter from Robert Carter to James Bradley, August 26, 1729

-1-

Rappa [hannock, Lancaster County, Virginia]

Augst 26. 1729

Mr: James Bradley

Sir

     The chief occasion of this is to desire a Small addition
of Goods may be made to those I have already sent to you for. (Vizt)
a close stool (besides that [illegible] in my former invoice to be round all of Wood Except the pan to have
a lock to it of this sort I have seen very handy ones;) A Set of Mill brasses
one for the Spindle two for the Gudgeons) Six dozen Strong mens plains
Common Shoes. 2 dozen 14s: 2 dozen 12s: 2 dozen 10s,) Six dozen large Irish Sto:
ckins.) fifty thousand 8s nails 30 thousand 10s nails;) 500 pounds of Iron for hoes in flat
Bars 1/2 for weeding hoes) Half a dozen pound of Powder for periwigs
One quart perfumed oil for Ditto:) And a fashonable suit of broad
Cloth cloths for my self for the Winter of a grave Colour lined with Shalloon
Mr: How has my measures and knows how to fit me he took it in
Virginia and has also made me Clothes since I am become a great much sm
aller in Bulk than I was at that time, Also a pair of Milled Stockings
and a pair of Worstead Stockings to suit the Clothes I hope this letter will
get [to you in] time Enough for the goods to come away with the rest

     You will have an Account from all hands of the great
destruction in our Crops by the continual floods of rain we have had
this Summer which will make them much short of what they were last
year some reckon a third some a fourth some More I shall make no guess.
es this with our Liberty of Stemming must of Necessity influence the
Market Considerably and we hope for that already gone I am


Sir
Your most humble Servant

-2-


A Clause to be added to Mr. Bradleys letter August 26th. 1729
Sir
not many days ago our Governor has been so kind to give
my Son Charles (who is now at Mans Estate and I believe of whom you
have some Remembrance) a Commission for the Naval Officer's place
of our river he hath given Security here but Security must
Also be given to the Commissioners of the Customs for the due
discharge of this office I have wrote to Mr. Dawkins to be one
of his Securitys and he hath wrote to Mr Athawes to be the Other
I tell Mr. Dawkins any of the Merchants thate [sic] I Correspond with
I believe will be joined with Him particularly naming you
If there be Occasion I request it of you and I will be your
Counter Security

NOTES



Source copy consulted: Letter book, 1728 August-1731 July, Robert Carter Papers (acc. no. 3807), Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library, University of Virginia. There is a nineteenth-century copy of this letter in the Minor-Blackford Papers, James Monroe Law Office and Museum, Fredericksburg, Virginia.

Thhe numerous parens seem to havve been entered by Carter's clerk, but probbably for some other reason than parenthetical expressions because they are not used to enclose sets of words.

Robert Carter generally used a return address of "Rappahannock" for the river on which he lived rather than "Corotoman," the name of his home, on his correspondence, especially to merchants abroad. The county and colony have been added for clarity.

[1] James Bradley was a London merchant with whom Carter dealt from at least 1723 until his death. As noted in his letter to Bradley of 1727 May 17, Bradley owned the Welcome, but little information about Bradley has been located.

[2] A close stool was a type of toilet with a padded seat and a removal pan beneath.

[3] Shalloon is a closely woven woolen used chiefly for linings. ( "Of Silk, Cotton, Linen and Wool." 18c New England Life: Clothing & Accoutrements. http://www.18cnewenglandlife.org/18cNEL/ofsilk.htm. 1/8/04)

[4] Parliament had passed an act forbidding the importation of stemmed tobacco in 1722. John Randolph was sent to England in 1729 as agent for Virginia to try to have the act overturned; his mission would be successful. ( Arthur Pierce Middleton. Tobacco Coast: A Maritime History of the Chesapeake Bay in the Colonial Era. [Newport News, VA: Mariners' Museum, 1953.] p. 116.)

[5] the Rappahannock
